# Roastery Lecture Notes: From Green Bean to Final Cup

## The Origin of Our Current Batch

On March 14, 2024, we received a 60-kilogram shipment of washed-process Arabica from Finca El Mirador in Huehuetenango. The farm sits at 1,850 meters above sea level, which slows cherry maturation and concentrates sugars. Jorge Méndez, the mill manager, fermented the lot for 22 hours before drying it on raised beds for 11 days. These variables explain the pronounced jasmine aroma and the clean, citrus finish we are seeing in the cupping bowl today.

## Roast Profile Development

Our production roaster, the Loring S35 Peregrine, handles batches up to 35 kilograms. For this Huehuetenango lot we are running a development ratio of 22 percent, reaching first crack at 196 °C and dropping the beans at 208 °C. The total roast time is 10 minutes and 45 seconds. If we push the development past 25 percent, the bergamot notes collapse into generic caramel. Keep the airflow at 75 percent through the Maillard phase to preserve acidity.

## Sensory Benchmarks and Brewing Protocols

Last Tuesday’s calibration session produced the following targets for baristas using the EK43 grinder set to 7.2:

- Dry fragrance: white tea and orange blossom
- Wet aroma: honey and toasted almond
- Flavor: lime zest, brown sugar, black tea
- Aftertaste: medium-long, with a 14-second lingering sweetness
- Body: silky, scoring 7 out of 10 on our tactile scale

We recommend a 1:16.5 ratio, 93 °C water, and a 3-minute 15-second total contact time on the flat-bed dripper.

## Storage, Resting, and Next Steps

Rest the roasted coffee for 72 hours before retail packaging. Nitrogen-flushed bags extend shelf stability to 90 days without noticeable staling. The next lecture, scheduled for October 3, will cover our new Ethiopian natural-process lot from the 2024 harvest. Read the green grading report from September 18 and bring questions about moisture content, which tested at 10.8 percent on arrival.
