# Incident Postmortem: Cryogenic Storage Failure — Lab 4B

## Summary
On March 14, 2024, at 02:17, the primary ultra-low temperature freezer in Lab 4B (Unit ULT-09) experienced a catastrophic compressor failure. The internal temperature rose from -80°C to -42°C over 18 hours before alarms reached on-call staff. The incident compromised 47 cryovials containing primary human hepatocyte cultures tied to Project Helix, led by Dr. Elena Vasquez.

## Timeline
- **02:17** — Compressor fault triggers local audible alarm; network relay fails silently.
- **08:45** — Morning technician notices frost melt and reports to facilities.
- **11:03** — Dr. Vasquez authorizes emergency liquid nitrogen backup transfer.
- **14:30** — All salvageable vials relocated to backup ULT-11; 12 vials declared nonviable.

## Root Cause Analysis
The failure originated from a seized scroll compressor due to inadequate lubrication after a deferred maintenance window. The monitoring gateway (Model TG-400) dropped its heartbeat at 02:19, but the alert routing table pointed to a deprecated Slack webhook rather than the current on-call roster. Consequently, no human was notified until physical inspection.

## Impact
- Loss of 12 irreplaceable donor-derived vials (Batch HPT-2024-031).
- Delay of Project Helix Phase II by approximately 6 weeks.
- Direct material and labor costs estimated at $84,000.

## Corrective Actions
- Replace ULT-09 compressor and recalibrate sensors by April 2, 2024.
- Migrate all alert endpoints to the new PagerDuty integration with SMS fallback.
- Implement redundant temperature telemetry using independent LoRa sensors.
- Conduct quarterly maintenance audits on all cryogenic units across Building C.

## Lessons Learned
Biological assets depend on mechanical vigilance. A single deprecated webhook erased the safety margin of an 18-hour failure window. Going forward, every critical storage unit must have dual-path notification and documented maintenance within 90-day cycles. Dr. Vasquez’s rapid triage protocol prevented total loss, confirming that clear escalation paths save science even when automation fails.
