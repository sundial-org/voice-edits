# Grant Proposal: Precision Epigenome Editing for Inherited Metabolic Disorders

## Executive Summary

Dr. Elena Vasquez and the team at Novagen Therapeutics seek $450,000 over 24 months to develop a CRISPR-dCas9 epigenetic platform targeting inherited hypercholesterolemia. Our preliminary data, published in *Nature Biotechnology* in March 2024, demonstrate 78% transcriptional silencing of mutant PCSK9 alleles in human hepatocyte models without double-strand breaks. By January 2026, we aim to complete preclinical safety profiling and submit an IND-enabling package to the FDA. This proposal outlines a rigorous path from vector optimization to non-human primate validation.

## Specific Aims

The central hypothesis is that targeted histone deacetylation at the PCSK9 locus can durably reduce circulating LDL cholesterol. We will test this through three integrated objectives:

- Engineer lipid-nanoparticle formulations compatible with primary human hepatocytes by Q3 2025
- Quantify off-target chromatin modifications via whole-genome bisulfite sequencing across 12 tissue types
- Establish dose-response relationships in cynomolgus macaques using a standardized 28-day infusion protocol

Each milestone feeds directly into our regulatory strategy for Phase I initiation in 2027.

## Methodology and Timeline

Year one focuses on vector refinement and in vitro validation. We will synthesize 14 guide-RNA variants and screen them in iPSC-derived hepatocyte spheroids, measuring LDL uptake via fluorescent cholesterol analogs. In parallel, our bioinformatics group—led by Dr. Samuel Okonkwo—will deploy a proprietary pipeline to predict chromatin accessibility at 8,000 potential off-target sites.

Year two shifts to large-animal studies. Twelve cynomolgus macaques will receive escalating doses of LNP-encapsulated dCas9-KRAB mRNA. Serum lipid panels, liver enzymes, and histopathology will be assessed weekly. All animals will undergo a 90-day recovery period to evaluate epigenetic persistence and reversibility.

## Budget and Impact

The $450,000 budget allocates 60% to animal studies and GMP-grade reagent synthesis, 25% to personnel (one postdoc and one research associate), and 15% to sequencing and regulatory consulting. If successful, this platform could extend beyond hypercholesterolemia to treat familial amyloidosis and urea-cycle defects, representing a transformative shift in precision medicine. We respectfully request your support to advance this program toward clinical reality.
